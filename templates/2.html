Business Confidence Index

The Business confidence Index, or BCI (Business Confidence Index), is a basic estimate of the state of the economy.

A negative value suggests that most companies are not optimistic about their country's economic prospects.

Business confidence indices have generally been developed and applied in developed countries.

As can be seen from the figure,

the Business confidence Index of four countries is listed, and the circle is used to show the size of the Index,with the size as the radius of the circle.

It can be seen that Brazil is the highest at 102.9, followed by India at 99.5, and Egypt is the third, with a gap of about 38, and Indonesia is the smallest at 54.54.
It can be seen that currently in Brazil, people have the highest information index on domestic,
which drives external and internal investment, and consumers are very optimistic about the prospect of this country.

It is worth noting that none of the four countries except India has a negative commercial consumption index due to the epidemic,
which indicates that the epidemic prevention and control is relatively good.

Let us compare the policy strategies among these four countries to find the reason behind:

The first plot is for policy response in India

In the beginning India brought the epidemic under control while enacting several restrictions, but the high intensity of the restrictions simultaneously led to a decline in the economy,
so in May the Indian government enacted an economic stimulus, while easing entry restrictions in June.

However,

It is not hard to find that the confirmed case is exponentially increasing around mid of June. This situation is closely related with the policy that easing the traveling restriction twice.


The second plot is for policy response in Brazil

There are very many similarities between Brazil and India in this outbreak, such as the fact that they both have very high numbers of infections.
The graph below shows that the number of infections in Brazil also started in June. An analysis of Brazil's policies shows that at the beginning of the outbreak,
Brazil implemented many policies to stimulate the economy, but not many policies to restrict the outbreak, including not declaring a state of emergency,
